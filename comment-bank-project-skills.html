Project ManagementBreaking projects into tasksPlanning with awareness of memory and workloadYou plan your work with an understanding of how memory works—using external systems to track tasks and keep key information accessible rather than relying on working memory alone.When planning a project, don't rely on remembering what needs to happen. The last 10% of any project—the edits, the logistics, the surprises—reliably takes 50% of the time.
Project ManagementBreaking projects into tasksUsing planning toolsYou use planning tools—calendars, Gantt charts, to-do lists, Kanban boards—consistently to plan and track your work across a project's full arc.Find a planning tool that works for you and use it consistently throughout a whole project. The value is in the habit: a Kanban board or calendar only helps if it stays current.
Project ManagementBreaking projects into tasksEstimating and prioritizing timeYou estimate how long tasks will take with accuracy and prioritize your time so the most critical work gets done first.Practice estimating how long each task will actually take, then track how close you were. Use that feedback to improve future estimates. Prioritize ruthlessly—not everything on the list matters equally.
Project ManagementTaking productive risksUnderstanding risk and rewardYou approach risk thoughtfully—understanding that taking risks can lead to both losses and gains, and weighing both sides rather than defaulting to caution or recklessness.Before taking a risk on a project, think it through: what's the potential gain? What's the potential loss? What's the cost of not taking it? Thoughtful risk-taking is different from either avoidance or recklessness.
Project ManagementTaking productive risksEvaluating feasibility of goalsYou evaluate the feasibility of your goals before committing to them, setting targets that are ambitious but grounded in a realistic assessment of your resources and time.Before finalizing a goal for a project, test its feasibility: do you have the time, resources, and knowledge to pull it off? Ambitious goals are valuable—but only when they're grounded in honest self-assessment.

Expect to move back and forth between stages rather than through them in a straight line.

A sketch, mock-up, or rough draft lets you test your assumptions early—while changing course is still easy.

Project ManagementPrototyping, testing, and iterationIterating through repeated revisionYou revise your work repeatedly, treating each iteration as a source of information that shapes the next version rather than as a failure to get it right the first time.Build repeated revision into your process as a feature, not a fix. Project ManagementDocumenting processExternalizing memory through documentationYou understand that memory consolidation requires externalizing your process—and you document your work knowing you won't reliably remember the details tomorrow.Build documentation into your process from the start, not at the end. Notes, photos, and logs aren't just records—they're how you consolidate what you've learned and make it available to your future self.
Project ManagementDocumenting processLogging work with photos and notesYou log your work as you go—taking photos, writing notes, and recording decisions—so your process is visible, traceable, and useful to return to.As you work, log it: take photos of drafts and prototypes, jot down what you tried and why, note what you changed. Don't wait until the end—the details that make documentation useful are the ones you lose first.

Identify specifically what failed and why, then use that insight in the next iteration. Failure is only wasted if you don't learn from it.
Project ManagementReflecting on progressJournalingYou journal regularly about your project progress, using writing to process what's working, what isn't, and what you're learning along the way.Build brief journaling into your project routine. Write about your progress, your frustrations, and your discoveries—writing forces clarity and often surfaces insights that thinking alone doesn't reach.
