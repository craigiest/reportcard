Project ManagementBreaking projects into tasksPlanning with awareness of memory and workloadYou plan your work with an understanding of how memory works—using external systems to track tasks and keep key information accessible rather than relying on working memory alone.When planning a project, don't rely on remembering what needs to happen. The last 10% of any project—the edits, the logistics, the surprises—reliably takes 50% of the time.
Project ManagementBreaking projects into tasksUsing planning toolsYou use planning tools—calendars, Gantt charts, to-do lists, Kanban boards—consistently to plan and track your work across a project's full arc.Find a planning tool that works for you and use it consistently throughout a whole project. The value is in the habit: a Kanban board or calendar only helps if it stays current.
Project ManagementBreaking projects into tasksEstimating and prioritizing timeYou estimate how long tasks will take with accuracy and prioritize your time so the most critical work gets done first.Practice estimating how long each task will actually take, then track how close you were. Use that feedback to improve future estimates. Prioritize ruthlessly—not everything on the list matters equally.
Project ManagementTaking productive risksUnderstanding risk and rewardYou approach risk thoughtfully—understanding that taking risks can lead to both losses and gains, and weighing both sides rather than defaulting to caution or recklessness.Before taking a risk on a project, think it through: what's the potential gain? What's the potential loss? What's the cost of not taking it? Thoughtful risk-taking is different from either avoidance or recklessness.

Ambitious goals are valuable—but only when they're grounded in honest self-assessment.
