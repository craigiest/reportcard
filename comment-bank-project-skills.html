Idea GenerationAnalyzing objects and problemsDistinguishing fact, opinion, and assumptionYou clearly distinguish between facts, opinions, and assumptions—in your own thinking and in the sources you engage with.Practice identifying what is fact, what is opinion, and what is assumption—in sources you read and in your own reasoning. Idea GenerationAnalyzing objects and problemsObservation, deduction, inference, and speculationYou move carefully between what you observe, what you can deduce, what you can infer, and what you're speculating—keeping these distinctions clear in your reasoning.When analyzing something, be explicit about your reasoning type: is this an observation or a deduction? Idea GenerationAnalyzing objects and problemsMind mappingYou use mind maps effectively to externalize and organize your thinking, making connections visible across a problem or topic.Use mind mapping as a thinking tool, not just a summary tool. Start with a central idea and let associations branch outward—the goal is to discover connections you didn't know you saw.
Idea GenerationAnalyzing objects and problemsCreating schematics and flowchartsYou create clear schematics and flowcharts that make systems, processes, and relationships visible and easier to reason about.Practice representing complex processes or systems as schematics or flowcharts. The act of drawing the diagram often reveals gaps and connections that aren't visible in prose.
Idea GenerationAnalyzing objects and problemsApplying skepticismYou apply genuine skepticism to what you read, hear, and conclude—questioning assumptions and looking for what might be missing or wrong.Practice healthy skepticism: when a source or claim seems convincing, ask what evidence would prove it wrong, who benefits from the claim, and what it leaves out. Skepticism is a tool for better thinking, not just an attitude.
Idea GenerationBranching and narrowing topicsDivide to multiplyYou use the "divide to multiply" principle—breaking a topic into subcategories to generate more and better options than a single broad approach would allow.When a topic feels too big or too vague, divide it into subcategories and explore each one. Division generates options that wouldn't be visible from the top level.
Idea GenerationBranching and narrowing topicsGenerating subcategories, examples, and combinationsYou generate rich sets of subcategories, examples, and combinations when exploring a topic, going beyond the obvious to find less expected possibilities.Push beyond the first examples and subcategories that come to mind. Ask: what are the intersections? What combinations haven't been tried? What's buzzing in adjacent spaces?

Use it to systematically expand your view of what's possible before narrowing to what's most promising.
Idea GenerationBranching and narrowing topicsGenerating topic treesYou generate detailed topic trees that map the branching possibilities within a subject, giving yourself a visual overview of the terrain before choosing a direction.Practice building a topic tree before committing to a direction. Map the branches, sub-branches, and intersections—seeing the full terrain helps you choose a path with more confidence.

Idea GenerationGenerating new ideas by building on existing ideasWorking within constraintsYou understand the paradox of constraints—that limits can inspire more creative thinking—and you use restrictions productively rather than treating them as obstacles.The next time a project has tight constraints, lean into them rather than resisting. Ask what becomes possible within the limits rather than focusing on what you've lost by having them.
